Section 1

The Pectoral Girdle

The clavicle and scapula link the upper limb to the axial skeleton through a single bony joint, sacrificing stability for extreme range of motion.

Plate 01

Pectoral Girdle (Anterior)

Anterior view of the bones associated with the pectoral girdle: the clavicle, scapula, and humerus shown in articulation with the axial skeleton.

Description
The pectoral girdle is the bony framework that connects the upper limb to the axial skeleton. Strictly, it consists of the clavicle and scapula. The humerus is the proximal limb bone that articulates with the girdle at the glenohumeral joint.
Location
Spans the upper thorax. The clavicle articulates medially with the manubrium and laterally with the acromion. The scapula sits posterolaterally on the rib cage. The humerus extends from the glenoid fossa downward.
Function
Provides extreme mobility for the upper limb at the cost of joint stability. Connects the limb to the trunk and serves as an attachment platform for muscles of the shoulder, neck, and trunk.
Special Structures
Clavicle, scapula, humerus.
Clinical Pearl
A floating girdle on a single bony connection
The pectoral girdle is unusual in that it has only one true bony connection to the axial skeleton: the sternoclavicular joint. The rest of the girdle floats on muscle attachments alone, allowing the wide range of motion at the shoulder but contributing to its instability.
Plate 02

Pectoral Girdle (Posterior)

Posterior view of the same three bones, emphasizing the position of the scapula on the dorsal thorax.

Description
Posteriorly, the scapula is the dominant feature of the upper back. It rests against ribs 2 through 7 and is attached to the trunk only by muscles, allowing it to glide as the arm moves.
Location
The scapula sits over the posterior thorax, with the medial border about 5 to 6 cm lateral to the spinous processes when the arm is at rest.
Function
Posterior view emphasizes the relationship between the scapula, the ribs, and the muscles of the back. Movement of the scapula (elevation, depression, protraction, retraction, upward and downward rotation) accompanies almost every shoulder motion.
Special Structures
Clavicle (visible at superior margin), scapula with its prominent spine, humerus.
Clinical Pearl
Scapular winging
Scapular winging refers to the medial border of the scapula lifting away from the rib cage when the arm pushes forward against resistance. The most common cause is paralysis of the serratus anterior due to long thoracic nerve injury, often from blunt trauma, surgical injury during axillary lymph node dissection, or repetitive overhead activity.
Plate 03

Joints of the Upper Extremity

The four joints essential to upper extremity function, linking the limb to the axial skeleton and to itself.

Sternoclavicular (SC) Joint
The only true bony attachment of the upper limb to the axial skeleton. A saddle-type synovial joint with a fibrocartilaginous disc, formed between the sternal end of the clavicle and the manubrium.
Acromioclavicular (AC) Joint
A plane synovial joint between the acromial end of the clavicle and the acromion of the scapula. Reinforced by the strong coracoclavicular ligament. AC separation (separated shoulder) is a common contact-sport injury.
Glenohumeral Joint
The shoulder joint proper. A multiaxial ball-and-socket synovial joint between the head of the humerus and the glenoid fossa of the scapula. The most mobile joint in the body, and consequently the most frequently dislocated.
Manubriosternal Joint
A symphysis (cartilaginous joint) between the manubrium and the body of the sternum. Marked externally by the sternal angle (angle of Louis), a useful landmark at the level of the second costal cartilage and the T4 to T5 disc.
Section 2

The Clavicle

A slender S-shaped strut, easy to palpate, common to fracture, and the only bony bridge from limb to trunk.

Plate 04

Clavicle

The clavicle, an S-shaped strut connecting the upper limb to the trunk and one of the most commonly fractured bones in the body.

Description
The clavicle is a long, slightly S-shaped bone running horizontally between the manubrium of the sternum and the acromion of the scapula. It is the only bone connecting the upper limb to the axial skeleton.
Location
Subcutaneous along its entire length, easily palpated above the first rib at the base of the neck.
Function
Acts as a strut that holds the scapula and arm laterally away from the thorax, allowing the upper limb to swing freely. Transmits force from the upper limb to the axial skeleton.
Special Structures
Sternal end (medial, rounded, articulates with manubrium), acromial end (lateral, flattened, articulates with acromion), conoid tubercle (inferior surface near the acromial end, attachment for the conoid ligament).
Clinical Pearl
The most commonly fractured bone
The clavicle is the most commonly fractured bone in the body, especially in newborns during difficult deliveries and in falls onto an outstretched arm or shoulder. The middle third is the most common fracture site because it is the thinnest portion and lies between the strong ligaments at each end.
Section 3

The Scapula

The triangular shoulder blade glides on the posterior thorax, anchoring the rotator cuff and providing the shallow socket of the shoulder joint.

Plate 05

Scapula (Anterior View)

Anterior (costal) view of the scapula showing the broad subscapular fossa and the projecting coracoid process.

Description
The scapula is a flat triangular bone covering the posterolateral upper thorax over ribs 2 through 7. The anterior surface faces the rib cage and is dominated by the subscapular fossa.
Location
Lies between the spine and the lateral chest wall, suspended on the thorax by the muscles of the rotator cuff and shoulder girdle.
Function
Anchors muscles that move the upper limb and transmits forces from the humerus to the clavicle. The subscapular fossa houses the subscapularis muscle (a member of the rotator cuff).
Special Structures
Acromion, coracoid process, glenoid fossa (cavity), subscapular fossa, suprascapular notch, superior border, superior angle, medial border, lateral border, inferior angle.
Clinical Pearl
The coracoid process: three muscles, three ligaments
The coracoid process is a key surgical landmark and the attachment site for three muscles (short head of biceps brachii, coracobrachialis, pectoralis minor) and three ligaments (coracoacromial, coracohumeral, coracoclavicular). The mnemonic "three muscles, three ligaments" captures all six structures.
Plate 06

Scapula (Posterior View)

Posterior view of the scapula showing the dividing spine and the supraspinous and infraspinous fossae that house two of the rotator cuff muscles.

Description
The posterior surface of the scapula is divided by a transverse ridge, the spine of the scapula, into two unequal fossae: the smaller supraspinous fossa above and the larger infraspinous fossa below.
Location
Faces dorsally, palpable through the skin of the upper back. The spine of the scapula and the acromion are easily felt.
Function
Houses two of the four rotator cuff muscles. Supraspinatus arises from the supraspinous fossa, infraspinatus from the infraspinous fossa. The spine of the scapula provides attachment for the trapezius and deltoid.
Special Structures
Spine of scapula, supraspinous fossa, infraspinous fossa, acromion (lateral end of spine), glenoid fossa, superior angle, inferior angle, medial border, lateral border, suprascapular notch, coracoid process.
Clinical Pearl
The rotator cuff: SITS muscles
Three of the four rotator cuff muscles (Supraspinatus, Infraspinatus, Teres minor) attach to the greater tubercle of the humerus. Subscapularis (the fourth) attaches to the lesser tubercle. Mnemonic: SITS muscles. The supraspinatus tendon is the most commonly torn, especially with overhead activities and in the elderly.
Plate 07

Scapula (Lateral View)

Lateral view of the scapula showing the glenoid fossa in profile, with the acromion above and the coracoid process projecting anteriorly.

Description
The lateral aspect of the scapula presents the glenoid fossa, the shallow socket that articulates with the head of the humerus to form the shoulder joint.
Location
Faces laterally toward the proximal humerus. The acromion projects superiorly and laterally; the coracoid process projects anteriorly and laterally.
Function
The shallow glenoid fossa permits enormous range of motion at the cost of stability. The fossa is deepened by the glenoid labrum, a fibrocartilaginous rim, and stabilized dynamically by the rotator cuff.
Special Structures
Glenoid fossa, acromion (superior, projects laterally over the joint), coracoid process (anterior, projects forward), lateral border.
Clinical Pearl
Shoulder dislocation and the Bankart lesion
The shoulder is the most frequently dislocated major joint. Anterior dislocation accounts for over 95 percent of cases because the anterior capsule is thinnest and the bony glenoid offers least resistance to anteroinferior translation. The Bankart lesion (avulsion of the anteroinferior labrum) is a common consequence and predisposes to recurrent dislocations.
Section 4

The Humerus

The long bone of the arm, with a head that articulates at the shoulder and a complex distal end that hinges and pivots at the elbow.

Plate 08

Humerus (Anterior View)

Anterior view of the humerus, the single bone of the arm, showing the head, the surgical neck, and the distal articular surfaces.

Description
The humerus is the longest bone of the upper limb, extending from the shoulder to the elbow. It articulates proximally with the glenoid fossa and distally with the radius and ulna.
Location
Spans the arm between shoulder and elbow.
Function
Provides attachment for the muscles of the shoulder, arm, and forearm. Transmits force from the shoulder to the forearm during pushing, pulling, lifting, and throwing.
Special Structures
Head, anatomical neck, surgical neck, greater tubercle, lesser tubercle, intertubercular (bicipital) groove, shaft, medial and lateral epicondyles, capitulum (lateral, articulates with the radial head), trochlea (medial, articulates with the trochlear notch of the ulna). Anteriorly, the coronoid fossa lies above the trochlea and the radial fossa above the capitulum.
Clinical Pearl
Surgical neck fracture and the axillary nerve
The surgical neck of the humerus is the most common humeral fracture site, especially in elderly patients with osteoporosis after a fall onto an outstretched arm. The axillary nerve and posterior circumflex humeral artery wrap around the surgical neck and are at risk. Look for deltoid weakness and loss of sensation over the regimental badge area on the lateral shoulder.
Plate 09

Humerus (Posterior Aspects)

Posterior aspects of the humerus, including the proximal tubercles, the radial groove of the shaft, the deltoid tuberosity, and the distal articulations.

Description
The humerus presents distinct features on its anterior and posterior surfaces. Posteriorly, the most important features are the radial (spiral) groove on the shaft, the olecranon fossa above the trochlea, and the ulnar groove on the posterior surface of the medial epicondyle.
Location
Faces dorsally.
Function
The radial groove transmits the radial nerve and deep brachial artery as they pass from the posterior compartment to the lateral arm. The olecranon fossa accommodates the olecranon of the ulna during full elbow extension. The ulnar groove transmits the ulnar nerve.
Special Structures
Greater tubercle and lesser tubercle (separated by the intertubercular groove), anatomical neck, surgical neck, deltoid tuberosity, radial (spiral) groove, lateral and medial supracondylar ridges, lateral and medial epicondyles, olecranon fossa (posterior, distal), ulnar groove (posterior surface of the medial epicondyle).
Clinical Pearl
Wrist drop and the funny bone
Mid-shaft humeral fractures can damage the radial nerve as it traverses the radial groove, producing wrist drop (loss of wrist and finger extension) and a small sensory deficit on the dorsum of the hand. The classic funny bone sensation comes from striking the ulnar nerve where it lies superficially in the ulnar groove behind the medial epicondyle.
Section 5

The Forearm

The ulna anchors the elbow; the radius pivots around it during pronation and supination.

Plate 10

Ulna

The ulna, the medial bone of the forearm, with a detailed proximal view showing the trochlear notch that articulates with the trochlea of the humerus.

Description
The ulna is the medial and longer of the two forearm bones. It is the principal bone at the elbow joint, where its trochlear notch grips the trochlea of the humerus in a stable hinge.
Location
Medial side of the forearm in anatomical position.
Function
Forms the primary articulation at the elbow (trochlear notch with the humeral trochlea), allowing flexion and extension. The proximal radio-ulnar joint at the radial notch permits the radius to rotate, producing pronation and supination.
Special Structures
Olecranon (the prominent point of the elbow), trochlear notch (articulates with the trochlea), coronoid process, radial notch (for proximal articulation with the radial head), ulnar tuberosity (insertion of brachialis), styloid process at the distal end.
Clinical Pearl
Olecranon bursitis (student's elbow)
Olecranon bursitis is inflammation of the bursa overlying the olecranon, often from prolonged pressure (leaning on the elbow at a desk) or repeated minor trauma. It causes a soft, swollen, often painless mass over the point of the elbow without restricting joint motion. Aspiration is sometimes needed to rule out septic bursitis.
Plate 11

Radius

The radius, the lateral bone of the forearm, with the disc-shaped articular fovea of the head and the prominent radial tuberosity.

Description
The radius is the lateral bone of the forearm. Unusually, its head sits at the proximal end on a short neck. The radius rotates around the ulna during pronation and supination.
Location
Lateral side of the forearm in anatomical position.
Function
The radius bears most of the weight transmitted from the hand to the elbow. The radial head pivots within the radial notch of the ulna and the annular ligament during pronation and supination of the forearm.
Special Structures
Radial head, articular fovea (concave superior surface of the head, articulates with the capitulum), radial tuberosity (insertion of biceps brachii tendon), shaft, styloid process (distal, lateral), ulnar notch (medial distal surface, articulates with the ulna at the distal radio-ulnar joint).
Clinical Pearl
Colles fracture and the dinner-fork deformity
A Colles fracture is a transverse fracture of the distal radius, typically from a fall on an outstretched hand (FOOSH), with characteristic dorsal angulation that creates a dinner-fork deformity of the wrist on lateral view. It is one of the most common fractures in postmenopausal women and is a sentinel injury for osteoporosis screening.
Section 6

The Hand

Twenty-seven bones giving humans their unmatched precision grip, fine manipulation, and opposable thumb.

Plate 12

The Hand

Color-coded view of the hand showing the three regions of the manus: carpus (wrist), metacarpus (palm), and phalanges (fingers).

Description
The hand contains 27 bones organized into three groups: 8 carpal bones in two rows at the wrist, 5 metacarpals forming the palm, and 14 phalanges in the digits.
Location
Distal segment of the upper limb, articulating with the radius and ulna at the wrist.
Function
Performs the precision grip and power grip required for human manipulation, tool use, and communication. The opposable thumb (digit I) is uniquely capable in its range of motion and dexterity.
Special Structures
Carpals (proximal row: scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum, pisiform; distal row: trapezium, trapezoid, capitate, hamate). Metacarpals I to V. Phalanges: each finger has proximal, middle, and distal phalanges, except the thumb (digit I) which has only proximal and distal (no middle).
Clinical Pearl
Ape hand deformity and median nerve injury
Ape hand deformity results from median nerve damage and atrophy of the thenar muscles, leaving the thumb unable to oppose. The thumb sits in the same plane as the fingers, like a non-human primate hand. This dramatically impairs precision grip and is a hallmark of severe carpal tunnel syndrome or proximal median nerve injury.
Plate 13

Carpal Bones

Color-coded view of the eight carpal bones arranged in two rows of four, classified by location and articulations.

Scaphoid
Largest of the proximal row. Boat-shaped. Forms the radial border of the wrist. The most commonly fractured carpal.
Lunate
Crescent-shaped, sits between the scaphoid and triquetrum. Articulates superiorly with the radius. Most commonly dislocated carpal.
Triquetrum
Pyramid-shaped, on the medial side of the proximal row.
Pisiform
Pea-shaped sesamoid bone embedded in the flexor carpi ulnaris tendon. Sits on the palmar surface of the triquetrum.
Trapezium
Most lateral of the distal row. Articulates with metacarpal I (thumb). Has a saddle-shaped joint that gives the thumb its opposition.
Trapezoid
Smallest bone of the distal row. Articulates with metacarpal II.
Capitate
Largest carpal. Sits centrally in the distal row. Articulates with metacarpal III.
Hamate
Most medial of the distal row. Has a hook (hamulus) on its palmar surface. Articulates with metacarpals IV and V.
Clinical Pearl
Mnemonic for the eight carpals and the scaphoid pitfall
Mnemonic: "Some Lovers Try Positions That They Cannot Handle" (Scaphoid, Lunate, Triquetrum, Pisiform, Trapezium, Trapezoid, Capitate, Hamate), going lateral to medial in the proximal row, then lateral to medial in the distal row. The scaphoid is the most commonly fractured carpal (FOOSH injury). Its blood supply enters distally, so proximal pole fractures risk avascular necrosis and nonunion. Pain in the anatomical snuffbox after a wrist injury is scaphoid fracture until proven otherwise.
