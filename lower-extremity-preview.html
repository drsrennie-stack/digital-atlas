Section 1

The Pelvic Girdle

The bony basin that joins the lower limb to the axial skeleton, supports the abdominal viscera, and forms the birth canal in females.

Plate 01

The Coxal Bone (os coxae)

Lateral view of a single hip bone, the large irregular bone of the pelvic girdle formed by the fusion of three bones during adolescence.

Description
The coxal bone, also called the hip bone or os coxae, is one of two large irregular bones that join the sacrum to form the bony pelvis. Each adult coxal bone is the fusion of three bones, ilium, ischium, and pubis, that meet at the acetabulum.
Location
Forms the lateral and anterior walls of the pelvic cavity. Articulates posteriorly with the sacrum at the sacroiliac joint, anteriorly with its counterpart at the pubic symphysis, and laterally with the head of the femur at the acetabulum.
Function
Bears and transmits the weight of the trunk to the lower limbs in standing and walking, anchors muscles of the abdomen, back, and thigh, and protects the pelvic viscera.
Special Structures
Iliac crest, ASIS and AIIS (anterior superior and inferior iliac spines), PSIS and PIIS (posterior superior and inferior iliac spines), acetabulum, obturator foramen, greater and lesser sciatic notches, ischial spine, pubic tubercle.
Clinical Pearl
Iliac crest as bone marrow harvest site
The posterior superior iliac spine is the standard site for bone marrow biopsy in adults. Red marrow yield is high, the bone is easy to access through the skin, and the trajectory keeps the needle clear of major neurovascular structures.
Plate 02

The Complete Pelvis

Anterior view of the articulated bony pelvis showing the coxal bones joined to the sacrum at the sacroiliac joints and to each other at the pubic symphysis.

Description
The complete bony pelvis is a basin formed by two coxal bones joined posteriorly to the sacrum at the sacroiliac joints and joined to each other anteriorly at the pubic symphysis.
Location
Spans the inferior end of the trunk, between the lumbar vertebral column and the lower limbs.
Function
Transfers weight from the axial skeleton to the lower limbs, supports and protects the pelvic and lower abdominal organs (bladder, rectum, internal reproductive structures), and forms the birth canal in females.
Special Structures
Sacroiliac joint, iliac crest, ASIS, AIIS, sacrum, superior and inferior pubic rami, pubic symphysis, acetabulum, obturator foramen, ischial spine.
Clinical Pearl
Pelvic inlet and obstetric anatomy
The pelvic inlet (superior aperture) and outlet (inferior aperture) define the obstetric passage. Female pelves are characteristically wider, shallower, and have a larger subpubic angle (greater than 80 degrees) than male pelves to accommodate childbirth. The ischial spines mark the narrowest part of the birth canal.
Plate 03

Bones of the Pelvis

Color-coded anterior view of the pelvis distinguishing the five major regions of the pelvic ring.

Sacrum
Triangular bone formed by the fusion of five sacral vertebrae. Articulates superiorly with L5, laterally with both ilia at the sacroiliac joints, and inferiorly with the coccyx.
Ilium
The largest and most superior of the three coxal bones. Forms the broad upper portion of the hip and the iliac crest you can palpate at the waist.
Ischium
The posteroinferior portion of the coxal bone. Forms the bony prominence you sit on (ischial tuberosity) and contributes to the obturator foramen.
Pubis
The anterior portion of the coxal bone. Joins its counterpart at the pubic symphysis and contributes the superior and inferior pubic rami to the obturator foramen.
Pubic Symphysis
A cartilaginous joint connecting the two pubic bones in the midline. The interpubic disc is fibrocartilage that softens during pregnancy under hormonal influence (relaxin).
Plate 04

Components of a Single Coxal Bone

Lateral view of a single coxal bone color-coded by its three component bones, with named subdivisions.

Body of Ilium
The thicker inferior portion of the ilium that helps form the upper part of the acetabulum.
Wing of Ilium (Ala)
The broad, flared upper portion that gives the hip its characteristic shape. The iliac crest tops the wing.
Body of Ischium
The thicker portion of the ischium that helps form the posteroinferior part of the acetabulum.
Ischial Ramus
The slender extension of the ischium that joins the inferior pubic ramus to complete the obturator foramen.
Superior Pubic Ramus
The bar of pubic bone extending laterally from the body of the pubis to contribute to the acetabulum.
Inferior Pubic Ramus
The bar of pubic bone extending laterally and inferiorly to meet the ischial ramus.
Acetabulum
The deep cup-shaped socket formed by all three bones (ilium, ischium, pubis) where the head of the femur articulates.
Obturator Foramen
The large oval opening framed by the pubis and ischium. Mostly closed by the obturator membrane in life, with a small canal for the obturator nerve and vessels.
Greater Sciatic Notch
The deep concavity on the posterior border of the ilium and ischium. The piriformis muscle and sciatic nerve pass through the greater sciatic foramen formed here.
Section 2

The Femur and Patella

The longest bone in the body and the largest sesamoid bone, working together to extend the knee.

Plate 05

Femur (Posterior View)

Posterior view of the femur (the longest, heaviest, and strongest bone in the body) with detailed view of the distal end showing the knee articulation.

Description
The femur is the single bone of the thigh, extending from the hip joint to the knee. It is the longest and heaviest bone in the human body and supports the weight of the trunk through every step.
Location
Articulates proximally with the acetabulum at the hip joint and distally with the tibia and patella at the knee joint.
Function
Transmits body weight from the hip to the knee during standing and locomotion, and serves as the lever for the powerful muscles of the hip, thigh, and knee.
Special Structures
Head with fovea capitis, neck, greater and lesser trochanters, intertrochanteric crest (posterior), gluteal tuberosity, linea aspera (posterior shaft), lateral and medial supracondylar lines, popliteal surface, lateral and medial condyles, lateral and medial epicondyles, intercondylar fossa.
Clinical Pearl
Femoral neck fractures and avascular necrosis
Femoral neck fractures are common in older adults with osteoporosis. The blood supply to the femoral head travels along the neck, so displaced intracapsular fractures often disrupt this supply and lead to avascular necrosis (osteonecrosis) of the head. Treatment frequently requires hemiarthroplasty rather than fixation.
Plate 06

Femur (Anterior) and Patella

Anterior view of the femur paired with two views of the patella, the largest sesamoid bone in the body.

Description
The anterior femur shows fewer surface landmarks than the posterior aspect, since the linea aspera and supracondylar lines are posterior features. The patella is a sesamoid bone embedded within the quadriceps femoris tendon.
Location
The patella sits anterior to the distal femur, articulating with the patellar surface (trochlear groove) between the lateral and medial femoral condyles.
Function
The patella improves the mechanical advantage of the quadriceps by holding its tendon further from the joint axis, increasing the moment arm during knee extension. It also protects the anterior knee joint.
Special Structures
On the anterior femur: head, neck, greater trochanter, lesser trochanter, intertrochanteric line (anterior, distinct from the posterior crest), medial and lateral epicondyles, medial and lateral condyles. The patella has a base (superior), apex (inferior), and articular facets posteriorly.
Clinical Pearl
Patellar tracking and Q angle
The quadriceps angle (Q angle) describes the lateral pull of the quadriceps on the patella. A wider pelvis increases this angle, which is one reason patellofemoral pain and lateral patellar dislocation are more common in women. The vastus medialis obliquus actively resists this lateral tracking and is a focus of physical therapy.
Section 3

The Leg

The tibia bears the weight of the body; the slender fibula stabilizes the ankle and anchors muscles.

Plate 07

Tibia

Anterior view of the right tibia with a superior plateau view showing the articular surfaces for the femoral condyles.

Description
The tibia is the larger and more medial of the two leg bones. It is the weight-bearing bone of the leg and forms the prominent shin you can palpate along its anterior border.
Location
Extends from the knee to the ankle. Articulates proximally with the femoral condyles at the knee, laterally with the fibular head, and distally with the talus and fibula at the ankle.
Function
Transmits the weight of the body from the knee to the ankle. The medial malleolus forms the medial portion of the ankle mortise, providing stability against eversion.
Special Structures
Lateral and medial condyles, intercondylar eminence, tibial tuberosity (insertion of patellar ligament), anterior crest, fibular notch (lateral surface, articulates with fibula), medial malleolus.
Clinical Pearl
Osgood-Schlatter disease
Osgood-Schlatter disease is a traction apophysitis of the tibial tuberosity, common in adolescent athletes during growth spurts. Repeated traction from the patellar ligament inflames the developing apophysis, causing localized pain and a palpable bony bump that can persist into adulthood.
Plate 08

Fibula

The fibula, the slender lateral leg bone, is non-weight-bearing but provides essential muscle attachment and ankle stability.

Description
The fibula is a thin lateral bone of the leg, extending parallel to the tibia. It does not bear significant body weight and can be partially harvested for grafting.
Location
Lateral to the tibia. Articulates proximally with the lateral condyle of the tibia at the proximal tibiofibular joint and distally with the tibia and talus at the ankle.
Function
Provides attachment for muscles of the leg and forms the lateral malleolus, the lateral wall of the ankle mortise that prevents medial displacement of the talus.
Special Structures
Head (proximal, articulates with tibia), shaft, lateral malleolus (distal, palpable as the bony bump on the outer ankle).
Clinical Pearl
Lateral malleolus and inversion ankle injuries
The lateral malleolus is the most commonly fractured component of an ankle inversion injury. The Ottawa Ankle Rules guide imaging decisions: tenderness over the posterior edge or tip of either malleolus, or inability to bear weight, warrants an X-ray.
Section 4

The Foot

Twenty-six bones organized into arches that support, propel, and adapt to terrain during gait.

Plate 09

Foot: Bones Overview

Color-coded view of the foot showing all 26 bones organized into three regions: tarsals (7), metatarsals (5), and phalanges (14).

Calcaneus
The largest tarsal bone. Forms the heel and bears the body weight transmitted from the talus to the ground.
Talus
Sits atop the calcaneus and articulates with the tibia and fibula at the ankle. The only tarsal that articulates with the leg bones.
Navicular
Boat-shaped bone medially, articulating with the talus posteriorly and the three cuneiforms anteriorly.
Cuboid
Cube-shaped tarsal on the lateral side, articulating with the calcaneus posteriorly and metatarsals IV and V anteriorly.
Cuneiforms (Medial, Middle, Lateral)
Three wedge-shaped tarsals between the navicular and metatarsals I, II, and III. They form the transverse arch of the foot.
Metatarsals (I to V)
Five long bones forming the body of the foot, numbered medial to lateral. Each has a base (proximal), shaft, and head (distal).
Proximal Phalanges (I to V)
The first row of toe bones, articulating with the heads of the metatarsals.
Middle Phalanges (II to V)
Present in toes 2 through 5 only. The great toe (digit I) has no middle phalanx.
Distal Phalanges (I to V)
The terminal toe bones bearing the toenails on their dorsal surfaces.
Plate 10

Tarsals

Detailed color-coded view of the seven tarsal bones forming the posterior half of the foot and the medial and longitudinal arches.

Calcaneus
The heel bone. Largest and strongest tarsal. Receives the calcaneal (Achilles) tendon posteriorly and articulates with the talus superiorly and the cuboid anteriorly.
Talus
Articulates with the tibia and fibula above (ankle joint), the calcaneus below, and the navicular in front. Has no muscle attachments. Its blood supply is precarious.
Navicular
Boat-shaped bone on the medial midfoot. Sits between the talus and the three cuneiforms. The tibialis posterior tendon inserts on its tuberosity.
Cuboid
Cube-shaped lateral tarsal. Articulates with the calcaneus posteriorly and the bases of metatarsals IV and V anteriorly. Has a groove for the peroneus longus tendon on its plantar surface.
Medial Cuneiform
Largest of the three cuneiforms. Articulates anteriorly with the base of metatarsal I.
Middle (Intermediate) Cuneiform
Smallest cuneiform. Articulates anteriorly with the base of metatarsal II.
Lateral Cuneiform
Articulates anteriorly with the base of metatarsal III. The lateral border articulates with the cuboid.
Clinical Pearl
Mnemonic for the seven tarsals
"Tiger Cubs Need MILC" gives the bones from posterior to anterior: Talus, Calcaneus, Navicular, Medial cuneiform, Intermediate (middle) cuneiform, Lateral cuneiform, Cuboid. Or for the first three plus the cuneiforms in order: "The Cuban Needs MILC."
Plate 11

Foot: Lateral and Articulated View

Oblique view of the foot showing the longitudinal arch and the relationship of all foot bones in articulation, from heel to toe.

Description
The foot is a flexible architectural structure of 26 bones organized into longitudinal and transverse arches that absorb shock and propel the body forward during gait.
Location
Distal segment of the lower limb, articulating with the tibia and fibula at the ankle joint (talocrural joint).
Function
Provides stable support during stance, adapts to uneven terrain, absorbs shock, stores and releases elastic energy during gait, and acts as a rigid lever during propulsion (toe-off).
Special Structures
Tarsals (calcaneus, talus, navicular, cuboid, three cuneiforms), metatarsals I to V, proximal phalanges I to V, middle phalanges II to V, distal phalanges I to V. Note that the great toe (digit I) has only two phalanges, while toes 2 through 5 have three each.
Clinical Pearl
Calcaneal "Lover's fracture" and the spine
A calcaneal fracture from jumping or falling onto the heels (sometimes called a "Lover's fracture") is associated with concomitant lumbar compression fractures because the axial force transmits up the spine. Always image the lumbar spine when a calcaneal fracture is found, even in the absence of back pain.
